# Topic Selection Logic

Use this before choosing a “观潮” topic. Do not select topics only because they are hot. Select topics that can reveal a second-order game.

## Core Standard

A topic is strong only when it can answer:

- What is the visible event?
- What is the hidden transfer of power?
- What second-order consequence does it trigger?
- Which countries, companies, suppliers, investors, and ordinary people are forced to react?
- Which old advantage may decay, and which new advantage may rise?

If the answer stops at “company X released product Y,” reject or deepen the topic.

## Second-Order Consequence Lens

Every selected topic should be pushed one layer deeper:

- Smart glasses are not just product releases. Ask whether visual entrance control changes supply-chain bargaining power, app distribution, advertising inventory, public-space data, and platform sovereignty.
- Apple supply-chain stories are not just “move out of China.” Ask whether Japan’s old precision-manufacturing advantages are being hollowed out, whether Southeast Asia or India can truly replace Chinese industrial clusters, and how China should respond by moving from assembly to components, standards, equipment, and scenario ecosystems.
- Humanoid robots are not just robots entering factories. Ask whether China is copying the EV playbook: low-cost components, fast iteration, local supply-chain depth, export pressure, and downstream scenario capture.
- AI power stories are not just “data centers use electricity.” Ask whether the US bottleneck is generation, grid interconnection, transformers, local opposition, regulation, power-market pricing, or energy sovereignty. Then compare whether China’s advantage is grid construction speed, industrial policy, energy siting, coal flexibility, renewables buildout, or integrated planning.

## Apple / Asia Supply Chain Story

Do not write “Apple supply chain is Americanized” unless sources prove it.

Better story frame:

> Apple may want to Americanize control of the human visual entrance, but the device’s body may still depend on Asian manufacturing, optical, display, chip, and assembly networks.

Deep questions:

- Which parts are US-controlled: operating system, chip design, app store, payment, identity, AI services, platform rules?
- Which parts remain Asian-dependent: final assembly, optics, displays, sensors, PCBs, batteries, precision components, packaging, TSMC fabrication?
- Is Japan declining from a full-stack electronics power into a high-end component supplier, or does it still hold chokepoints such as optics, materials, sensors, and precision equipment?
- Are India and Southeast Asia replacing China, or mostly taking final assembly while key components and engineering depth remain tied to China/Taiwan/Japan/Korea?
- Does China’s破局 move from “make the product” to “own the component, equipment, standard, scenario, and domestic platform ecosystem”?

## Japan Decline / China Response Lens

When a topic touches Japan, do not reduce it to decline.

Ask:

- Is Japan losing final-product brands but retaining upstream chokepoints?
- Is Japan shifting from visible consumer electronics to invisible materials, sensors, optics, machine tools, and precision equipment?
- Is China replacing Japan in full products, or still dependent on Japanese upstream components?
- How does China break through: domestic substitution, acquisition of process know-how, scenario scale, engineering talent, standards, and export ecosystems?

## Southeast Asia / India Replacement Lens

Do not treat manufacturing relocation as full replacement.

Separate:

- assembly capacity
- supplier density
- engineering iteration speed
- component localization
- logistics and port efficiency
- labor quality and management depth
- tariff and policy incentives
- dependence on Chinese managers, machines, components, and materials

Strong angle:

> Southeast Asia and India may become Apple’s risk hedge, but whether they become a true replacement depends on whether they can grow from assembly islands into dense industrial ecosystems.

## AI Power Sovereignty Lens

For AI electricity topics, ask what the US is really trying to solve:

- slow grid interconnection
- transformer and turbine shortages
- aging transmission lines
- local opposition and moratoriums
- power-price backlash
- mismatch between data-center speed and utility planning cycles
- dependence on gas, nuclear restarts, private power clusters, batteries, and flexible load management

Compare with China carefully:

- China may have advantages in grid construction speed, state coordination, renewables deployment, coal backup, and industrial siting.
- The US may have advantages in private capital, gas resources, nuclear restarts, deregulated market experimentation, and hyperscaler purchasing power.
- Do not write a simple “US behind / China ahead” story. The better story is two systems solving the same bottleneck with different weapons.

Story frame:

> AI power war is not only about who has more electricity. It is about who can connect power, land, cooling, chips, models, permits, and capital into one deployable infrastructure machine.

## Ranking Additions

Add these to the scoring model:

- `second_order_depth`: Does the topic trigger a bigger hidden consequence?
- `supply_chain_tension`: Is there a contradiction between platform control and manufacturing dependence?
- `regional_replacement_risk`: Does it raise the question of China, Japan, India, Southeast Asia, US, or Europe being replaced or forced to upgrade?
- `system_competition_depth`: Does it reveal two national or industrial systems solving the same problem differently?
- `story_character_strength`: Can the players be written as characters with motives, fear, and bargaining chips?

Prefer topics scoring high on at least three of these five.
